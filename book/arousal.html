pMSE Book · Part II · Network 1

Arousal

LC-NE / ARAS · substrate: locus coeruleus–norepinephrine + ascending reticular activating system · classical term: arousal / vigilance
Arousal is the brain's global activation level — the gain set by the locus coeruleus–norepinephrine system and the ascending reticular activating system. Its negative pole is hypoarousal (stupor, somnolence, shutdown); its positive pole is hyperarousal (startle, agitation, sustained over-alertness). It is intensity, not content.

Definition

We score arousal, the global level of bodily activation set by Network 1 — the activation/gain layer of the LC-NE / ARAS system. The calibrated function (the healthy midpoint) is a patient who is awake, available, and activated in proportion to context. We use arousal in a deliberately bedside sense — observable activation, intensity rather than content — not as a single biological assay.

It does not capture its neighbors. It is not Consciousness (Network 1: clarity of the sensorium, not its intensity — a patient can be hyperaroused yet clear, or hypoaroused yet interpretable — and the disorders-of-consciousness literature treats it as its own graded level: Giacino et al., 2018; Kondziella et al., 2020); not Threat (Network 4: the affective meaning of danger, not the activation it rides on); not Anticipation/drive (Network 5: activation becomes drive only when goal-directed); not Psychomotor speed (Network 6: tempo of output, not global gain); and not Sleep (Network 2: a temporal pattern, not the present state).

Neuroscience

The substrate is an ascending neuromodulatory system, not a connectivity network: the locus coeruleus is the principal source of cortical norepinephrine; the ascending reticular activating system with the thalamus sustains cortical activation; hypothalamic orexin/histamine circuits govern sleep–wake transitions; and the autonomic system expresses peripheral activation. Arousal is not unitary: multiple neuromodulatory systems converge on cortical arousal on different timescales — basal-forebrain parvalbumin neurons produce rapid microarousals, while LC-NE and basal-forebrain acetylcholine act more slowly but sustain vigilance (Maness et al., 2022). Computationally, arousal can be read as the brain's global precision, or gain — the weight given to incoming prediction errors (Barrett & Simmons, 2015).

We read this substrate along three aspects: tonic level (the baseline), phasic reactivity (the response to input) — the tonic/phasic distinction drawn from the LC-NE literature (Aston-Jones & Cohen, 2005; Feld & Feige, 2021) — and regulation, the stability of that level over time, where the vigilance regulation model proposes that stability, not just height, carries diagnostic weight: mania as unstable arousal countered by restlessness, depression as tonically high, rigidly sustained vigilance (Hegerl & Hensch, 2014). established the substrate & these reading-aspects. pMSE inference that a single bedside two-pole read indexes them.

The two axes

Negative axis · hypofunction

Hypoarousal

When LC-NE/ARAS output falls, the system cannot reach or sustain threshold; the bedside read is hypoarousal.

Umbrella: hypoarousal
→ somnolence, obtundation, lethargy, and stupor — a state of minimal reactivity (Berrios, 1981), classically divided into depressive, catatonic, and manic stupor. In manic stupor, inner activation runs high while observable reactivity collapses — the dissociation that is why we score observable arousal as its own layer.

established the substrate. pMSE inference treating full stupor as a composite (low observable arousal + abolished drive [Network 5] + motor immobility [Network 6]), which we decompose rather than score as one sign.

Positive axis · hyperfunction

Hyperarousal

When gain is high, any input — a noise, a glance, a memory — is amplified; the bedside read is hyperarousal.

Umbrella: hyperarousal
→ sub-terms that remain global: sustained over-activation, exaggerated startle, and global hypervigilance/scanning. The seven empirically separable subtypes — anxious, somatic, sensitive, sleep-related, irritable, vigilant, sudomotor (Bresser et al., 2026) — are real but redistribute to neighbor items (see False friends); arousal keeps only the global level.

established the substrate & the seven-dimension structure. pMSE inference keeping only the global level here and redistributing the subtypes.

Across the poles
Level alone does not separate every syndrome: two patients can share a high positive-pole level and differ entirely in regulation (see Neuroscience) — so the bedside read notes not just the pole but its stability. We treat the specific mood-disorder predictions as a model under test, not settled fact.

Assessment & cross-walk

Assessment. For the negative pole: does the patient stay awake without stimulation? response latency and rousability; posture, tone, drowsiness. For the positive pole: startle, scanning, reactivity to noise/light/waiting, and modulability — "let's slow the breathing" — can they bring it down? For both: does the activation have a goal, a fear, or is it just level? (redirect to Network 5/4/6 if so). Score the observable current state plus recent examples.

False friends. Fear/anxiety → Threat (Network 4). Goal-directed activity → Anticipation/drive (Network 5). Purposeless motor restlessness → Psychomotor speed (Network 6)/akathisia. Altered clarity → Consciousness (Network 1). Insomnia as a pattern → Sleep (Network 2). Arousal keeps only the global level.

Validity

Construct. Arousal indexes a single observable layer — global activation — distinct from clarity (Consciousness), affect (Threat), and tempo (Psychomotor). Its bidirectionality is itself a claim: the most comprehensive transdiagnostic arousal instrument to date, the 27-item Transdiagnostic Hyperarousal Dimensions Questionnaire (THDQ), is unipolar — it scores seven hyperarousal dimensions from 0 (absence) to 4, with no hypoarousal pole, and its expert panel explicitly set catatonia aside as outside the construct (Bresser et al., 2026). The negative axis of this item fills precisely the pole the field's best instrument leaves empty.

Convergent (plausible). The poles should track autonomic measures, actigraphy, and hyperarousal scales. Divergent. Arousal should separate from Threat and from Consciousness; a hyperaroused, non-fearful, clear patient is the test case.

Predictive. The negative pole appears in hypoactive delirium, melancholia, catatonia, and intoxication; the positive pole in anxiety, PTSD, mania, insomnia, stimulant use, and psychosis — by different mechanisms, so one diagnosis can carry opposite arousal profiles. At the level of the arousal system — not yet the bedside item — the prognostic signal is already real: a transdiagnostic EEG signature of frontal beta indexing hypoarousal and impulse control predicted remission to methylphenidate in ADHD and to antidepressants in depression across independent datasets (Meijs et al., 2024), and locus coeruleus integrity on neuromelanin-sensitive MRI predicted plasma pTau217, cortical atrophy, and cognitive decline in Alzheimer's disease (David et al., 2025). pMSE inference whether the bedside two-pole read inherits that weight is still open — and a caution holds, since some arousal-based treatment-prediction signals have failed confirmatory replication.

Limitations & future

The item compresses a multidimensional construct into two observable poles; it will not, on its own, distinguish anxious from somatic from sensory hyperarousal — by design, those redistribute to other items. Reliability of the two-pole reading is undetermined and is the first validation target, alongside convergence with autonomic and actigraphic measures, and the eventual question of whether the tonic, phasic, and regulation aspects deserve separate documentation.

Standing caution: this is a formulation, not a validated biomarker — a bedside read of system gain, not a physiological assay.
